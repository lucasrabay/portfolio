---
title: "On teaching Portuguese to migrants (and to machines)"
date: "2025-03-20"
description: "What building Warao taught me about language, empathy, and edge cases."
readingTime: "5 min read"
---

The Warao are one of the largest indigenous groups to migrate to Brazil in recent years, arriving mostly from Venezuela. They speak their own language, some Spanish, and very little Portuguese. When they arrive, the language barrier is not just inconvenient — it is isolating. It shapes whether they can find work, access healthcare, or enroll their children in school.

I named the project after them because they represent something broader: the experience of arriving somewhere and not having the words. Warao is a gamified web app that teaches Portuguese through adaptive exercises, contextual vocabulary, and game mechanics that make daily practice feel less like homework. The target users are migrants and refugees — people who are learning a language not as a hobby, but as a survival skill.

Building Warao forced me to confront assumptions I did not know I had. Most NLP tools treat Portuguese as a single thing, but Brazilian Portuguese is full of regional slang, informal constructions, and spoken patterns that do not appear in textbooks. A migrant learning Portuguese in Roraima hears a very different language than what standard datasets represent. We had to think carefully about which Portuguese we were teaching and who got to define "correct."

The most interesting lesson was not technical. It was about empathy as a design constraint. When you build for people who are exhausted, stressed, and navigating an unfamiliar country, you cannot afford a confusing interface or a punishing difficulty curve. Every friction point is a reason to stop. That pressure made Warao better — simpler, kinder, more focused. I think most software would improve if we designed it for the person having the worst day of their life.
