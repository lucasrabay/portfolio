Six months ago I was convinced that fine-tuning was the answer to every domain-specific NLP problem. Custom model, custom data, custom performance. The engineering purity of it was appealing.

Then I ran the numbers.

## The Fine-Tuning Tax

For a classification task we were building at work — categorizing support tickets into 47 internal routing labels — I spent two weeks preparing a fine-tuning dataset. 3,200 labeled examples, cleaned, validated, split into train/eval sets. Fine-tuned GPT-3.5 on the set. Accuracy: 89.2%.

Then a colleague wrote a system prompt with five few-shot examples and a structured output schema. Accuracy on the same eval set: 87.8%. Time spent: 45 minutes.

The 1.4% accuracy gap cost us two weeks of data engineering and an ongoing fine-tuning budget. For a ticket router. The prompt version was also easier to debug, update, and explain to stakeholders.

## When Fine-Tuning Wins

Fine-tuning is not dead. It wins when:

- **Latency is critical** and you need a smaller model to match a larger model's behavior
- **Cost at scale** makes per-token prompt overhead expensive (millions of daily calls)
- **Style transfer** requires the model to internalize a voice or format that few-shot examples cannot fully capture
- **Proprietary knowledge** cannot be put into a prompt for security or compliance reasons

For everything else — prototyping, internal tools, moderate-scale production systems — structured prompting with a capable base model is faster to ship, easier to iterate, and surprisingly competitive on quality.

## The Real Skill

The industry conversation frames this as "prompt engineering vs. fine-tuning" like they are competing approaches. They are not. They are tools with different cost curves. The real skill is knowing which cost curve you are on before you start building.

I still fine-tune when the problem demands it. But I start with prompting, measure the gap, and only cross over when the delta justifies the investment. Most of the time, it does not.
