Brazil has around 11 million functionally illiterate adults. That is not a stat you read and forget — it is a reality that shapes entire lives. People who cannot read a bus schedule, fill out a job application, or understand a work contract. The job market is not designed for them, and neither is most technology.

I started building Biu because I kept asking myself a simple question: if multimodal AI can understand images, speech, and text simultaneously, why are we only using it to summarize emails and generate marketing copy? The people who would benefit the most from an intelligent assistant — people who literally cannot read the interface — are the ones being ignored.

Biu works by combining vision models, speech recognition, and large language models into an agent that meets users where they are. You can photograph a job listing and Biu will read it aloud, explain the requirements, and help you draft a response — all through voice. No typing, no reading required. It is not a literacy app. It does not try to teach people to read. It accepts that right now, today, someone needs to apply for a job and should not be blocked by a PDF.

The hardest part was not the technical stack. LangChain, a few well-chosen models, and solid prompt engineering got us most of the way there. The hardest part was designing for dignity — making sure the experience felt empowering rather than patronizing. Every design decision went through the same filter: would this make someone feel capable, or would it remind them of what they cannot do? That question changed how I think about building software entirely.
